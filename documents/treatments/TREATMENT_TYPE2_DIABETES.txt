DISEASE_OVERVIEW
Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus represents the most common form of diabetes (90-95% of cases). Management varies significantly based on comorbidities, BMI, renal function, and cardiovascular risk. This document presents three distinct clinical presentations with divergent treatment strategies within the same diagnostic category, enabling comparison of therapeutic approaches and outcomes.

- Weight management (especially if BMI > 30)
- Renal function preservation
- Cardiovascular risk reduction

SECTION_A_PUBLIC
Disease Overview & Clinical Context

Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus is a metabolic disorder characterized by insulin resistance and progressive pancreatic beta-cell dysfunction. Management requires individualized treatment selection based on:
- Patient comorbidities (renal disease, cardiovascular disease, obesity)
- Baseline glycemic control (HbA1c)
- Symptom burden and complications
- Patient preference and tolerability

This section presents three real-world patients with the SAME diagnosis (Type 2 DM) but DIFFERENT clinical presentations, requiring DIFFERENT treatment strategies.

PATIENT_CASE_001

case_id: P-001-T2DM
patient_identifier: Anonymized Patient 001
primary_diagnosis: Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus with Peripheral Neuropathy
icd_codes: E11, G63 (neuropathy complication)
baseline_hba1c: 9.2% (poorly controlled)
baseline_bmi: 28 (overweight)
comorbidities: Hypertension, Peripheral Neuropathy, prior_myocardial_infarction

Clinical Presentation - Symptoms
- Polyuria (frequent urination, especially nocturia)
- Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
- Tingling and numbness in both feet (peripheral neuropathy)
- Generalized fatigue and low energy
- Blurred vision (early diabetic retinopathy indicator)
- Slow wound healing on lower extremities

Clinical Context
Patient has long-standing poorly controlled diabetes with neuropathic complications and cardiovascular risk. Requires rapid glycemic control to prevent progression of microvascular complications.

Treatment Regimen
- Primary: Metformin 1000 mg twice daily (total 2000 mg/day)
- Secondary: Insulin Glargine 20 units at bedtime (basal insulin for fasting control)
- Adjunctive: Paracetamol for neuropathic pain; Pregabalin 150 mg OD for neuropathic symptoms

Rationale
- Metformin: First-line agent; cardioprotective; addresses insulin resistance
- Insulin: Rapid glucose reduction needed; HbA1c 9.2% indicates strong advantage of basal-bolus therapy for neuropathy prevention
- Combination: Aggressive glycemic control to prevent further neuropathic progression

Treatment Advantages (Pros)
✓ Rapid and potent glucose control
✓ Well-established safety profile across decades of use
✓ Metformin reduces cardiovascular risk
✓ Flexible dosing; insulin can be titrated upward with simple calculations

Treatment Disadvantages (Cons)
✗ Hypoglycaemia risk (especially fasting with basal insulin)
✗ Weight gain pattern with insulin therapy
✗ Daily injection burden; lower patient adherence risk
✗ Requires education on carbohydrate counting and injection technique
✗ Need for regular blood glucose monitoring (fingerstick or CGM)

Clinical Outcome
- 12-week HbA1c: 7.8% (reduction of 1.4%)
- Weight change: +1.5 kg (modest weight gain)
- Neuropathy symptoms: Stable; some improvement in pain with pregabalin
- Adverse events: One mild hypoglycaemic episode (fasting 68 mg/dL, self-treated)
- Assessment: MODERATE success; acceptable glycemic improvement with manageable hypoglycaemia risk

PATIENT_CASE_002

case_id: P-002-T2DM
patient_identifier: Anonymized Patient 002
primary_diagnosis: Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus with Obesity
icd_codes: E11, E66 (obesity complication)
baseline_hba1c: 8.5% (moderately controlled but suboptimal)
baseline_bmi: 34 (obese)
comorbidities: Obesity, Hypertension, NAFLD (non-alcoholic fatty liver disease), Acanthosis Nigricans

Clinical Presentation - Symptoms
- Excessive hunger and postprandial fatigue (meals cause energy crashes)
- Frequent infections (slower wound healing; thrush episodes)
- Acanthosis nigricans (dark, velvety skin patches on neck/axillae—insulin resistance marker)
- Dyspnoea on exertion (obesity-related)
- Sleep apnoea symptoms (daytime somnolence)

Clinical Context
Patient has significant insulin resistance (evidenced by acanthosis nigricans and BMI 34). Weight loss is as important as glycemic control. Rapid-acting agents and weight-loss benefits are priority.

Treatment Regimen
- Primary: Semaglutide 1 mg subcutaneously once weekly
- Secondary: Metformin 500 mg twice daily (total 1000 mg/day, lower than Case 001)
- Adjunctive: Lisinopril 10 mg OD for hypertension; Atorvastatin 40 mg OD for lipid control

Rationale
- Semaglutide (GLP-1 agonist): Powerful weight loss agent (3-5 kg expected); cardiovascular protection; once-weekly convenience
- Metformin: Lower dose sufficient with GLP-1; reduces hepatic glucose production
- Combination: Addresses both glycemic control AND obesity; high patient satisfaction

Treatment Advantages (Pros)
✓ Significant weight loss (expected 4-8 kg in 12-16 weeks)
✓ Once-weekly dosing improves adherence vs. daily pills
✓ Strong cardiovascular benefit (reduces MI/stroke risk)
✓ Very low hypoglycaemia risk (physiologic insulin secretion preserved)
✓ Improves lipid profile and liver enzymes (NAFLD improvement)

Treatment Disadvantages (Cons)
✗ Nausea and vomiting initially (weeks 1-4, usually mild and transient)
✗ High acquisition cost (~Rs. 8,000-10,000 per month)
✗ Requires injection training; patient must remember weekly injection day
✗ Rare: Medullary thyroid carcinoma risk (absolute contraindication in personal/family hx of MTC)
✗ GI side effects can mean reduced appetite/oral intake (patient compliance challenge)

Clinical Outcome
- 16-week HbA1c: 7.1% (reduction of 1.4%)
- Weight change: −6 kg (significant weight loss achieved)
- Acanthosis nigricans: Gradual fading observed by 12 weeks
- Sleep apnoea: Mild improvement in daytime somnolence
- Adverse events: Nausea for first 3 weeks (mild); resolved without intervention
- Assessment: GOOD success; excellent glycemic control + weight loss + improved quality of life

PATIENT_CASE_003

case_id: P-003-T2DM
patient_identifier: Anonymized Patient 003
primary_diagnosis: Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus with Chronic Kidney Disease Stage 3a
icd_codes: E11, N18.1 (CKD Stage 3a)
baseline_hba1c: 8.0% (moderately controlled)
baseline_bmi: 26 (normal)
baseline_egfr: 52 mL/min/1.73m² (CKD Stage 3a)
comorbidities: Chronic Kidney Disease Stage 3a, Hypertension, Mild albuminuria (ACR 35 mg/g), Dyslipidaemia

Clinical Presentation - Symptoms
- Polyuria and mild nocturia (diabetes + CKD)
- Bilateral ankle oedema (CKD-related fluid retention; not severe)
- Persistent fatigue (CKD anaemia contribution)
- Mild hypertension (resistant to prior monotherapy)
- Slow-to-heal minor cuts/bruises (CKD anaemia + diabetes)

Clinical Context
Patient has early renal disease (eGFR 52). Standard metformin is contraindicated (eGFR <45). Requires renal-protective agents that also reduce albuminuria progression and preserve eGFR.

Treatment Regimen
- Primary: Empagliflozin 10 mg once daily (SGLT2 inhibitor)
- Secondary: Linagliptin 5 mg once daily (DPP-4 inhibitor, no renal dose adjustment needed)
- Adjunctive: Losartan 50 mg OD (ARB for renal/BP protection); Ferrous sulphate 325 mg OD (CKD anaemia)

Rationale
- Empagliflozin: SGLT2 inhibitor; renal-protective via multiple mechanisms; reduces eGFR decline by ~30-40%; cardioprotective
- Linagliptin: DPP-4 inhibitor; no renal dose adjustment; low hypoglycaemia risk; neutral on weight
- Combination: Avoids metformin (contraindicated); achieves glycemic control while slowing CKD progression

Treatment Advantages (Pros)
✓ Renal-protective; slows eGFR decline and albuminuria progression (evidence-based)
✓ Cardioprotective (SGLT2 inhibitors reduce CV death + HF hospitalizations)
✓ Both agents are ORAL (no injections; high adherence)
✓ Very low hypoglycaemia risk (physiologic insulin secretion preserved)
✓ Weight-neutral; no adverse weight changes
✓ Safe in advanced CKD (eGFR ≥20 mL/min; adjustments only at eGFR <20)

Treatment Disadvantages (Cons)
✗ SGLT2i risk: Urinary Tract Infections (UTI) and genital mycotic infections (10-15% incidence)
✗ Rare: Diabetic Ketoacidosis risk (manageable with patient education)
✗ Cost: Both agents are expensive in India (~Rs. 3,000-5,000/month combined)
✗ Requires monitoring: Urine ketones, genital hygiene education, annual urine culture screening
✗ Dose limitations: Empagliflozin not recommended if eGFR <20 mL/min (requires reassessment in future CKD progression)

Clinical Outcome
- 24-week HbA1c: 7.3% (reduction of 0.7% — slower but stable)
- eGFR: 51 mL/min/1.73m² (STABLE; prevented decline; typical CKD progression ~2-3 mL/min/year expected)
- Albuminuria: Reduced from 35 to 18 mg/g (improvement in kidney proteinuria)
- Blood Pressure: Improved to 128/78 mmHg (controlled on losartan + empagliflozin)
- Adverse events: One UTI episode at week 8 (treated with cephalexin); resolved
- Assessment: GOOD success; glycemic control MAINTAINED while achieving renal protection and CKD stabilization

DECISION FRAMEWORK FOR CLINICIANS

1. IF Patient has Neuropathic Complications + CVD Risk + Normal Renal Function
   → Approach: Aggressive glycemic control via basal insulin + metformin (Case 001 model)
   → Suitable: HbA1c > 9%, presence of microvascular complications, normal eGFR
   → Monitor: Hypoglycaemia; weight gain; neuropathic symptom progression

2. IF Patient has Significant Obesity (BMI > 30) + Suboptimal Control + No CKD
   → Approach: GLP-1 agonist (weekly) + metformin for dual benefit (Case 002 model)
   → Suitable: Weight loss is priority equal to glycemic control; high CV risk; motivated patient
   → Monitor: Nausea tolerance; weight trajectory; cost-benefit for long-term adherence

3. IF Patient has CKD Stage 3-4 + Microalbuminuria + Need for Renal Protection
   → Approach: SGLT2i + DPP-4i, avoiding metformin (Case 003 model)
   → Suitable: eGFR 20-59 (Stage 3-4); albuminuria present; need for long-term nephroprotection
   → Monitor: UTI; genital infections; eGFR trend; potassium levels (if CKD Stage 4)

SECTION_C_TREATMENT_EVIDENCE
Evidence-Based Outcomes & Literature Basis

Regimen 001 (Metformin + Basal Insulin)
- Reference: UKPDS 33; Diabetes Care 1998
- HbA1c reduction: typically 1.5-2% when combined
- Hypoglycaemia incidence: 5-10% with basal-only insulin
- Cardiovascular benefit: Modest (metformin reduces MI by ~18%)

Regimen 002 (Semaglutide + Metformin)
- Reference: SUSTAIN-6 trial (Semaglutide in Type 2 Diabetes); NEJM 2016
- HbA1c reduction: 1.5-1.8%
- Weight loss: 3-5 kg average; up to 10 kg in responsive patients
- CV outcomes: 26% reduction in CV death/MI/stroke

Regimen 003 (Empagliflozin + Linagliptin)
- Reference: EMPA-KIDNEY trial (2022); LANCET Diabetes Endocrinol
- HbA1c reduction: 0.8-1.0%
- eGFR protection: 28% slower decline vs placebo
- CV outcomes: 25% reduction in CV death/hospitalization in CKD cohorts
