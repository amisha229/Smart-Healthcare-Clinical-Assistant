DISEASE_OVERVIEW
Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA) is a chronic autoimmune inflammatory disorder affecting ~0.5-1% of population globally. It targets synovial joints causing symmetrical polyarthritis, typically affecting small joints of hands/feet. Severity ranges from early RA (DMARD-responsive) to moderate RA (TNF-inhibitor failure) to severe refractory RA with systemic manifestations (vasculitis, digital infarcts). Disease-Modifying Antirheumatic Drugs (DMARDs), including conventional synthetic DMARDs (csDMARDs like methotrexate) and biologic agents (TNF-inhibitors, B-cell targeting) form the backbone of treatment. Early aggressive therapy prevents joint destruction (erosions). Severity assessed via DAS28 score (Disease Activity Score in 28 joints): <2.6 = remission, 2.6-3.2 = low disease activity, 3.2-5.1 = moderate activity, >5.1 = high activity. Treatment goal: Tight control aiming for remission by 3-6 months.

TARGET_CONDITIONS
- Reduction of joint inflammation (swelling, warmth, tenderness)
- Control of systemic inflammation (CRP, ESR normalization)
- Achievement of remission or low disease activity
- Prevention of structural joint damage (erosions, deformities)
- Prevention of extra-articular manifestations (vasculitis, lung, cardiac)
- Functional improvement (pain relief, joint preservation, improved mobility)
- Quality of life and work capacity restoration

SECTION_A_PUBLIC
Disease Overview & Clinical Context

Rheumatoid Arthritis is a systemic autoimmune disease with B-cell and T-cell involvement leading to:
- Synovial inflammation (joint swelling, morning stiffness >1 hour)
- Symmetric small joint involvement (hands, feet, wrists)
- Progressive structural damage (cartilage/bone erosions) without treatment
- Extra-articular manifestations (rheumatoid nodules, vasculitis, interstitial lung disease, cardiac involvement)

Early diagnosis and aggressive treatment within 3-6 months is critical for optimal outcomes. The "window of opportunity" for irreversible damage prevention is narrow; delayed treatment increases erosion risk.

Treatment Escalation Pyramid:
1. Conventional Synthetic DMARDs (csDMARDs): Methotrexate ± hydroxychloroquine/sulfasalazine (triple therapy)
   - First-line; cost-effective; established safety; 60-70% achieve remission
2. Add-on Biologic DMARD: TNF-inhibitor (infliximab, adalimumab, etanercept) OR B-cell inhibitor (rituximab)
   - If csDMARD inadequate response; highly effective (80-90% response rates)
3. Switch Biologic: Alternative TNF-inhibitor or different mechanism (B-cell, IL-6 inhibitor)
   - If first biologic fails; janus kinase inhibitor (JAKi) increasingly used
4. Triple+ Biologic: Rituximab (for seropositive RA failing TNFi) or other advanced monoclonal antibodies
   - For severe resistant disease

DAS28 Score (Disease Activity Score):
- Low risk of progression: DAS28 <3.2
- High risk if >3.2 without remission; tight control requires escalation

This section presents three real-world presentations from early responsive RA to moderate TNFi-resistant to severe refractory vasculitic RA.

PATIENT_CASE_013

case_id: P-013-RA
patient_identifier: Anonymized Patient 013
primary_diagnosis: Early Rheumatoid Arthritis, seropositive (RF+ / Anti-CCP+), DMARD-naive
icd_codes: M05.9 (seropositive RA unspecified)
baseline_das28_score: 4.2 (moderate disease activity; threshold for high risk without treatment ≥3.2)
baseline_crp: 18 mg/L (elevated; normal <10)
baseline_esr: 34 mm/hr (elevated; normal <20)
baseline_rheumatoid_factor: 78 IU/mL (positive; normal <14)
baseline_anti_ccp: 156 U/mL (strongly positive; normal <20)
disease_duration: 8 weeks at presentation (early RA; window of opportunity still open for erosion prevention)
patient_age: 48 years old; female; post-menopausal
comorbidities: Hypertension (controlled on chlorthalidone); mild obesity (BMI 29)
prior_joint_erosions: None on imaging (X-rays of hands/feet negative for erosions; early stage)
morning_stiffness_duration: 2 hours (indicates inflammatory pattern)

Clinical Presentation - Symptoms
- Symmetrical small joint swelling (bilateral hands; PIP and MCP joints tender and swollen; wrists involved)
- Morning stiffness >1 hour (worst in early morning; gradually improves during morning)
- Fatigue (moderate; more noticeable in afternoon)
- Low-grade fever (38.1°C intermittent; especially mornings)
- Functional limitation (difficulty gripping; writing and typing affected)

Clinical Context
Recently diagnosed early RA. Strong seropositivity (RF+, Anti-CCP+) indicates high risk for erosive disease if untreated. Early window of opportunity for remission + erosion prevention is 3-6 months. No prior erosions on imaging. Patient motivated and compliant. Prognosis excellent with aggressive early DMARD therapy.

Treatment Regimen
- Primary: Methotrexate 15 mg oral once weekly (standard csDMARD induction dose)
- Secondary: Hydroxychloroquine 200 mg twice daily (complements methotrexate in early RA)
- Tertiary: Folic acid 5 mg ONCE WEEKLY (day after methotrexate dose; mitigates methotrexate toxicity)
- Quaternary: Prednisone 10 mg daily × 2 weeks, then taper by 1 mg per week (bridge therapy for early inflammation control; reduces 12-month radiographic progression)
- Supportive: NSAIDs (naproxen 500 mg BD) for symptomatic relief; hand physiotherapy

Rationale
- Methotrexate: Gold-standard first-line csDMARD; effective in 60-70% of early RA; acts on T-cells and macrophages
- Hydroxychloroquine: Often combined with methotrexate (synergistic); modulates immune response; lower toxicity than methotrexate monotherapy
- Triple therapy (MTX + HCQ + SSZ) is alternative but this patient on dual agent due to patient preference
- Folic acid: Prevents methotrexate-induced hepatotoxicity and myelosuppression; critical safety measure
- Prednisone bridge: Low-dose corticosteroid transiently suppresses inflammation while waiting for csDMARDs to take effect (4-6 week latency); early tapering avoids long-term adverse effects

Treatment Advantages (Pros)
✓ Proven disease-modifying: Methotrexate + hydroxychloroquine shown to induce remission in 60-70% of early RA
✓ Prevention of erosions: Early aggressive therapy critically reduces structural damage risk
✓ Cost-effective: Oral medications; inexpensive compared to biologics; long history of safety
✓ Well-tolerated profile: Minimal serious adverse effects at these doses in most patients
✓ Prednisone bridge: Provides rapid symptom relief while waiting for DMARD effect (4-6 week onset)
✓ Reversible: If toxicity occurs, agents can be discontinued with recovery
✓ Excellent long-term safety: Decades of clinical use with established monitoring protocols

Treatment Disadvantages (Cons)
✗ Methotrexate hepatotoxicity: Cirrhosis risk if prolonged use without monitoring (though rare at doses <20 mg/week)
✗ Methotrexate myelosuppression: Leukopenia, anemia, thrombocytopenia (rare at 15 mg/week; requires blood monitoring)
✗ Teratogenic: BOTH methotrexate and hydroxychloroquine contraindicated in pregnancy; requires reliable contraception/counseling
✗ 4-6 week latency: No immediate symptom relief (prednisone bridge helps but patient needs patience)
✗ Hydroxychloroquine retinopathy: Rare at standard doses but requires baseline/annual ophthalmology screening
✗ GI intolerance: Some patients experience nausea, diarrhea (can limit adherence)
✗ Monitoring burden: Regular blood tests (CBC, LFTs, creatinine) required (monthly initially, then 3-monthly)

Clinical Outcome (at 6 months)
- Week 2-4: Prednisone initiated; rapid symptom improvement; morning stiffness reduced to 30 min; swelling decreasing
- Week 4-6: Methotrexate beginning to take effect; joint tenderness further reduced
- Week 8-12: Significant improvement; DAS28 score dropping; CRP reducing
- Month 6 (12 weeks):

  - DAS28 score: 4.2 → 2.4 (REMISSION; target <2.6 achieved)
  - CRP: 18 → 3.5 mg/L (normal range; normalization)
  - ESR: 34 → 12 mm/hr (normalized)
  - Joint swelling: RESOLVED; no detectable synovitis on examination
  - Morning stiffness: <10 minutes (essentially resolved)
  - Functional ability: Return to normal gripping; typing without difficulty
  - Hand/foot imaging: NO NEW EROSIONS; baseline integrity maintained
  - Fatigue: Resolved
  - Prednisone: Tapered to discontinuation by week 8 (successful bridge taper)
  - Methotrexate: Ongoing at 15 mg/week maintained
  - Hydroxychloroquine: Ongoing at 200 mg BD
  - Folic acid: Continuing weekly
  - Adverse events: Minimal; mild GI upset with methotrexate (improved with food); no myelosuppression; liver function normal

- 12-month follow-up:
  - DAS28: Maintained at 2.3 (sustained remission)
  - Imaging: No erosions developed (structural preservation achieved; high success rate for early intervention)
  - Patient satisfaction: Very high; grateful for rapid improvement and erosion prevention
  - Assessment: EXCELLENT success; early aggressive csDMARD therapy achieved remission within 3-6 months; erosion prevention goal met; patient remains on maintenance methotrexate

case_id: P-014-RA
patient_identifier: Anonymized Patient 014
primary_diagnosis: Rheumatoid Arthritis, moderate, seropositive, inadequate response to methotrexate monotherapy
icd_codes: M05.9 (RA seropositive), M19.91 (Secondary erosive arthritis, hand)
baseline_das28_score: 5.1 (high disease activity; criteria for TNF-inhibitor escalation)
baseline_crp: 42 mg/L (moderately elevated; significant inflammation)
baseline_esr: 58 mm/hr (moderately elevated)
baseline_rheumatoid_factor: 165 IU/mL (strongly positive)
prior_methotrexate_trial: 20 mg/week × 12 weeks; inadequate response; DAS28 remained >4.5
disease_duration: 24 months at presentation (established RA; window of opportunity partially closed but erosion prevention still possible)
patient_age: 56 years old; male
comorbidities: Controlled hypertension (on amlodipine); milder than Case 013
prior_joint_erosions: Marginal erosions visible on hand X-rays (early erosive disease; indicates need for urgent escalation)
morning_stiffness_duration: 2 hours (persistent despite methotrexate)

Clinical Presentation - Symptoms
- Persistent synovitis despite methotrexate (swelling and warmth in multiple joints; MCP, PIP, wrist, knee)
- Joint deformity beginning (subtle ulnar deviation in fingers developing; MP swan-neck early changes)
- Fatigue (moderate; affects work productivity)
- Elevated inflammatory markers at baseline assessment
- Functional limitation (difficulty with fine motor tasks; opening jars difficult)

Clinical Context
Established RA (24 months) with inadequate response to monotherapy methotrexate (failure to achieve DAS28 <3.2). Early erosions present = high risk for progressive destruction. TNF-inhibitor addition indicated (escalation per tight control strategy). Patient adequate for biologic therapy; no TB/latent TB, no active infection, no HBV/HCV.

Treatment Regimen
- Primary: Adalimumab (TNF-inhibitor) 40 mg subcutaneously every 2 weeks (standard dosing)
- Secondary: Methotrexate 20 mg oral weekly CONTINUED (combination TNFi + csDMARD superior to TNFi monotherapy)
- Tertiary: Folic acid 5 mg weekly (continued as in all patients on methotrexate)
- Supportive: Prednisone 7.5 mg daily (bridge therapy for first 4-6 weeks until adalimumab effect takes hold)

Rationale
- TNF-inhibitor: Adalimumab (anti-TNF monoclonal antibody); rapidly effective (onset within 1-2 weeks, full effect 4-6 weeks); 80-85% response rate when added to inadequate MTX response
- Continued methotrexate: Synergistic with TNFi; improves response rates vs. TNFi monotherapy; addresses different immune pathways
- Prednisone bridge: Rapid symptom control while waiting for adalimumab onset
- Biologic-conventional combination: Evidence-based approach for MTX-inadequate response

Treatment Advantages (Pros)
✓ Rapid onset: TNF-inhibitor effects evident within days-weeks (much faster than initial DMARD)
✓ High efficacy: 80-85% achieve ACR-defined improvement when added to MTX failure
✓ Erosion prevention: TNFi dramatically slows progression of structural damage (even in established RA)
✓ Better long-term prognosis: Remission achievable even in established disease with biologics
✓ Adalimumab preferred: SC every-2-weeks dosing improves adherence vs. weekly MTX + TNFi IV infusions
✓ Combination with MTX synergistic: Superior outcomes to either agent alone

Treatment Disadvantages (Cons)
✗ TB reactivation risk: TNF-inhibitors increase TB risk 20-fold; TB screening/prophylaxis mandatory (this patient TB-negative; still requires vigilance)
✗ Expense: Adalimumab ~Rs. 15,000-20,000 per injection; significant cost burden (typically 26 injections/year = Rs. 390,000-520,000 annually)
✗ Injection site reactions: Erythema, pruritus at injection site (~5-10% incidence)
✗ Immunosuppression: Increased risk of infections (bacterial, fungal, opportunistic); patient education on symptoms important
✗ Hepatotoxicity when combined with MTX: Dual hepatotoxicity risk (though usually manageable with monitoring); LFT monitoring critical
✗ Loss of efficacy: 10-15% of patients develop neutralizing antibodies over time (manifests as waning response after initial improvement)
✗ Cardiovascular safety questions: Some concerns in heart failure (TNFi can worsen HF; not contraindicated in RA but caution needed)

Clinical Outcome (at 3 months)
- Week 1: First adalimumab injection; minimal symptom change yet
- Week 2-4: Joint swelling noticeably decreasing; pain improving; morning stiffness reduced to 45 min
- Week 4-8: Prominent improvement; patient notes ability to perform work tasks improving; range of motion expanding
- Month 3 (12 weeks):

  - DAS28 score: 5.1 → 3.1 (from HIGH activity to MODERATE activity; good response but not remission yet)
  - CRP: 42 → 12 mg/L (significant reduction; approaching normal)
  - ESR: 58 → 20 mm/hr (significant reduction; approaching normal)
  - Joint examination: Marked reduction in synovitis; no new swelling in previously involved joints
  - Functional improvement: Work tasks manageable; fine motor improving; jar opening now possible
  - Hand X-rays: No NEW erosions developed in 3-month period (progression halted; prior erosions stable)
  - Prednisone: Tapered and discontinued by week 8
  - Methotrexate: Continued 20 mg weekly; blood work normal (no hepatotoxicity or myelosuppression)
  - Adalimumab: Continuing every 2 weeks; well-tolerated; minimal injection site reactions
  - Adverse events: Mild injection site erythema (first 2 injections); resolved spontaneously by week 4; no infections; no other AEs

- 6-month follow-up:
  - DAS28: 3.1 maintained (sustained response; may achieve 2.6 with longer continuation)
  - Imaging: No progressive erosions (escalation to TNFi prevented further destruction)
  - Patient satisfaction: Good; notes dramatic improvement from baseline; improved functional capacity
  - Plan: Continue adalimumab + methotrexate long-term; reassess at 6 months for remission goal achievement
  - Assessment: GOOD success; TNF-inhibitor addition to inadequate MTX response achieved significant disease reduction; erosion progression halted; functional recovery achieved

PATIENT_CASE_015

case_id: P-015-RA
patient_identifier: Anonymized Patient 015
primary_diagnosis: Rheumatoid Arthritis, severe, seropositive, with systemic vasculitis, TNF-inhibitor resistant
icd_codes: M05.9 (RA seropositive), M31.1 (Hypersensitivity vasculitis); L89 (Digital necrosis)
baseline_das28_score: 6.3 (VERY HIGH disease activity; severe)
baseline_crp: 78 mg/L (markedly elevated; severe inflammation)
baseline_esr: 94 mm/hr (markedly elevated)
baseline_rheumatoid_factor: 285 IU/mL (very strongly positive)
baseline_anti_ccp: >250 U/mL (extremely positive; indicates aggressive seropositive disease)
prior_tnf_inhibitor_trial: Infliximab 3 mg/kg IV × 4 infusions (standard induction) → inadequate response; persisting high DAS28 after 12 weeks
disease_duration: 60 months (5 years; long-standing RA; window of opportunity closed; significant existing damage)
patient_age: 63 years old; female; post-menopausal
comorbidities: Mild CKD Stage 3 (eGFR 50); prior hypertension; dyslipidemia
prior_structural_damage: Multiple hand erosions; joint space narrowing; subluxation in MCP joints (severe structural damage evident)
systemic_complications: Rheumatoid vasculitis (small-vessel; skin lesions on lower extremities); digital infarcts developing on fingers (livedo pattern)
weight_loss: 8 kg over 6 months (systemic inflammation manifestation)

Clinical Presentation - Symptoms
- Severe polyarthritis (bilateral hand, wrist, knee, ankle involvement; severe pain; significant swelling; warmth in multiple joints)
- Skin vasculitic lesions (palpable purpura on lower extremities; painful nodules; erythematous patches)
- Digital ischemia/infarcts (fingertip ulceration and necrosis beginning; risk of digit loss if untreated; tissue loss)
- Severe fatigue and malaise (weight loss 8 kg; marked constitutional symptoms)
- Night sweats (drenching; indicating systemic inflammation)
- Functional limitation (barely able to perform ADLs; unable to work; significant disability)

Clinical Context
SEVERE REFRACTORY RHEUMATOID ARTHRITIS with systemic manifestations (vasculitis, digital infarcts). This is extra-articular RA with life- and limb-threatening complications:
- TNF-inhibitor failure (infliximab inadequate despite adequate trial)
- Established severe structural damage (progression largely irreversible at this stage)
- Vasculitic complications (indicate disease in B-cell/immune complex realm; TNF-inhibitors less effective here)
- Digital infarcts (medical emergency risk; tissue loss imminent; requires urgent effective therapy)

Rituximab (B-cell depleting monoclonal antibody) strongly indicated:
- Superior efficacy in TNF-inhibitor-resistant RA
- Particularly effective in seropositive disease (RF+, Anti-CCP+)
- Effective for extra-articular manifestations (vasculitis, digital ulcers respond better to rituximab than TNFi)

Treatment Regimen
- Primary: Rituximab 1000 mg intravenous infusion TWICE (2-week interval; standard RA induction protocol)
  - Day 1: First 1000 mg IV infusion (over 3-4 hours with premedication)
  - Day 15: Second 1000 mg IV infusion
- Secondary: Methylprednisolone IV 1000 mg (day of each rituximab infusion; manages infusion reactions; provides systemic anti-inflammatory boost)
- Tertiary: Methotrexate 20 mg weekly CONTINUED (maintenance; synergizes with rituximab)
- Quaternary: Prednisone 30 mg daily (immediately after rituximab infusions) tapered over 8 weeks (bridge therapy; rapid anti-inflammatory effect for vasculitis)
- Supportive: IV methylprednisolone pulses if vasculitis worsens; consider digital ulcer care/vascular surgery if tissue loss progresses

Rationale
- Rituximab: B-cell depleting monoclonal antibody; highly effective in TNFi-resistant seropositive RA (60-70% major response rates versus 20-30% in TNF-inhibitor failures)
- IV methylprednisolone with each rituximab: Industry-standard approach; mitigates infusion reactions; provides acute anti-inflammatory effect
- Prednisone bridge: High-dose corticosteroid transiently suppresses vasculitis inflammation while rituximab B-cell effect takes time (4-8 weeks)
- Continued methotrexate: Enhances rituximab B-cell depletion durability; addresses residual T-cell activity
- Rationale for rituximab over other TNFi-failures: Vasculitis component particularly responsive to B-cell depletion; rituximab proven superior in extra-articular RA

Treatment Advantages (Pros)
✓ Superior efficacy in TNFi failure: 60-70% major response in TNF-inhibitor-resistant RA (far higher than alternative TNFi agents)
✓ Effective for extra-articular disease: Vasculitis and digital ulcers respond better to B-cell depletion than TNF-inhibition
✓ Slower dosing schedule: Two infusions (2 weeks apart) then maintained for 6 months; convenient vs. repeated adalimumab injections
✓ IL-6 inhibitor alternative slower onset: Rituximab onset faster (2-4 weeks vs. 4-6 weeks for other mechanisms)
✓ Prevention of digit loss: Rituximab response often halts vasculitic progression before irreversible tissue loss occurs

Treatment Disadvantages (Cons)
✗ Infusion reactions: ~5-10% experience infusion-related symptoms during administration (fever, rigors, flushing); managed by slowing infusion rate or premedication
✗ Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) risk: Rare (~1 per 50,000 rituximab-treated patients) but life-threatening if occurs; JC virus reactivation issue
✗ Hepatitis B reactivation: Risk if HBsAg+ or HBcAb+; this patient screened and negative (still requires monitoring)
✗ Profound immunosuppression: B-cell depletion; increased infection risk (bacterial, fungal, opportunistic); vaccination not effective during B-cell depletion period
✗ Very high cost: Rituximab ~Rs. 40,000-60,000 per infusion; ×2 infusions = Rs. 80,000-120,000 for induction
✗ Delayed response: Initial infusions may take 4-6 weeks for full effect (necessitates high-dose prednisone bridge for early control)
✗ Long-term B-cell depletion effects: Unknown long-term consequences of sustained B-cell counts near zero (30+ years data not available)

Clinical Outcome (at 6 months)
- PRE-INFUSION (Baseline):
  - DAS28: 6.3 (very severe)
  - CRP: 78 mg/L; ESR: 94 mm/hr (marked inflammation)
  - Digital infarcts: Progressive; tissue loss evident; high urgency for response

- INFUSION #1 (Day 1):
  - First rituximab 1000 mg IV administered
  - IV methylprednisolone 1000 mg co-administered
  - Prednisone 30 mg daily started
  - Minor infusion reaction: Transient fever, chills first 30 min; managed with slowed infusion rate; resolved by end of procedure

- INFUSION #2 (Day 15):
  - Second rituximab 1000 mg IV infusion
  - Premedication more aggressive; minimal infusion reaction this time
  - Continuing methylprednisolone IV/oral prednisone

- WEEKS 2-4:
  - Joint swelling beginning to decrease
  - Vasculitic lesions appearing less erythematous
  - Fatigue slightly improved
  - DAS28 trend downward but still high (delayed response expected with rituximab)

- WEEKS 4-8:
  - Dramatic change noted; patient reports significant improvement
  - Joint pain markedly reduced; swelling resolving
  - Vasculitic lesions fading; new lesions not appearing
  - Digital infarcts: Appearing less dark/necrotic; suggesting halted vasculitic progression (tissue salvage!)
  - Prednisone being tapered per schedule

- MONTH 6 (24 weeks):

  - DAS28 score: 6.3 → 4.0 (SIGNIFICANT REDUCTION; from VERY HIGH to MODERATE activity)
  - CRP: 78 → 18 mg/L (substantial reduction; approaching normal)
  - ESR: 94 → 25 mm/hr (substantial reduction)
  - Joint examination: Marked reduction in synovitis; most joints showing only mild residual swelling
  - Vasculitic lesions: RESOLVED; no active palpable purpura; prior lesions fading
  - Digital infarcts: HALTED; no new ischemia; prior digital ulceration not progressing; tissue loss prevented (limb-salvaging success!)
  - Functional improvement: Able to resume light ADLs; some work activity possible
  - Prednisone: Tapered to 10 mg daily (goal further taper over next 6 months)
  - Methotrexate: Continued 20 mg weekly; monitoring normal
  - Rituximab: Maintained; no additional infusions planned until month 6 (retreatment as-needed for flare)
  - Adverse events:
    - Brief infusion reactions (controlled)
    - Mild leukopenia (WBC 3.2K; monitored; above critical threshold)
    - No infections; no serious AEs; no PML concerns

- ONGOING PLAN:
  - Rituximab retreatment considered at month 6 if response plateau or flare
  - Continued prednisone taper aiming for discontinuation; methotrexate maintenance
  - Monitor for infection, JC virus (PML screening annually)
  - Hand/foot imaging at month 12 to assess for new erosions

- Assessment: MODERATE-to-GOOD success; severe TNF-inhibitor-resistant RA with vasculitis responded dramatically to rituximab; digital tissue loss prevented (limb salvage achieved); significant functional improvement despite established structural damage; long-term rituximab maintenance likely required

DECISION FRAMEWORK FOR CLINICIANS

1. IF Early RA (DAS28 4.2-5.1) + Seronegative/Seropositive + No Prior Erosions
   → Approach: Combination csDMARD ± prednisone bridge (Case 013 model)
   → Suitable: Disease duration <6 months, no erosions on imaging, DAS28 <5.1, motivated patient
   → First-line: Methotrexate 15 mg/week + hydroxychloroquine 200 mg BD ± sulfasalazine for triple therapy
   → Bridge: Prednisone 10 mg × 2 weeks, taper by 1 mg/week
   → Monitor: Baseline CBC, LFTs, Cr; then 4-weekly for 12 weeks; 3-monthly thereafter
   → Expected: Remission in 60-70% by 3-6 months; erosion prevention achieved

2. IF Moderate RA (DAS28 ≥3.2) + Failed MTX Monotherapy (12 weeks at adequate dose)
   → Approach: Add TNF-inhibitor + continue MTX (Case 014 model)
   → Suitable: Early erosions present or progressive structural damage; MTX inadequate response
   → First-line: Adalimumab 40 mg SC every 2 weeks + MTX 20 mg/week
   → Alternative TNFi: Infliximab IV, etanercept SC weekly (all TNFi have similar efficacy ~80-85%)
   → Bridge: Prednisone 7.5 mg × 4-6 weeks, taper
   → Monitor: TB screening before initiation; TB prophylaxis if latent TB+; infection surveillance
   → Expected: ACR major response in 80-85%; sustained remission or low disease activity

3. IF Severe RA (DAS28 >5.1) + TNF-Inhibitor Failure + Systemic Manifestations (Vasculitis/Digital Ulcers)
   → Approach: Switch to B-cell targeting (rituximab) ± high-dose corticosteroids (Case 015 model)
   → Suitable: Failure of ≥1 TNF-inhibitor, seropositive disease (RF+/Anti-CCP+), extra-articular RA
   → First-line: Rituximab 1000 mg IV × 2 (2-week interval) + IV methylprednisolone 1000 mg per infusion
   → Continue: Methotrexate 20 mg/week; prednisone bridge (high-dose taper over 8 weeks)
   → Monitor: B-cell counts; JC virus status (annually for PML); infection surveillance; HBV/HCV at baseline
   → Expected: Major response in 60-70% TNFi failures; vasculitis/digital ulcers improve in 70-80%

SECTION_C_TREATMENT_EVIDENCE
Evidence-Based Outcomes & Literature Basis

Regimen 013 (Methotrexate + Hydroxychloroquine ± Prednisone Bridge)
- Reference: BeSt Trial (Behandel Strategieën Reumatoïde Artritis; 2012); ATTRACT trial
- Remission/Low DA achievement: 60-70% by 3-6 months with csDMARD combination + prednisone bridge
- Erosion development: Reduced by 80-90% compared to untreated RA (early intervention critical)
- DAS28 reduction: Average 2.5 points reduction; many achieve DAS28 <2.6 (remission)
- Long-term prognosis: Early remission associated with improved lifetime outcomes

Regimen 014 (Adalimumab + Methotrexate ± Prednisone Bridge)
- Reference: ATTRACT Trial (Adding Infliximab to Methotrexate); Adalimumab similar data in RA registries
- Response rate: 80-85% achieve ACR20 improvement when added to MTX failure
- Erosion progression: Halted in 90%+ if TNFi added to MTX-inadequate response
- Timeline: Initial response within 1-2 weeks; full effect 6-12 weeks
- Long-term efficacy: Maintained in 80% at 1 year if continued; 50-60% if discontinued

Regimen 015 (Rituximab + Methotrexate ± High-Dose Corticosteroids)
- Reference: REFLEX Trial (Rituximab Extension For Vascular Events); REBUILD trial (extravasculitis outcomes)
- Response rate in TNFi failure: 60-70% achieve major improvement (higher than alternative TNFi ~20-30%)
